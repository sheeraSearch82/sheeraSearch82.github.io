F*, Low* and Pulse — Project-Based
Advanced Undergraduate / Postgraduate / Research

One of very few educators globally who can teach F*, Low*, and Pulse — cutting-edge proof-oriented programming languages actively developed at Microsoft Research — from a position of published, peer-reviewed research. Students learn not from documentation but from the decisions, dead-ends, and proof strategies behind a real verified system.

Hoare Logic and Separation Logic
Advanced Undergraduate / Postgraduate

A dedicated course on the two most important logics for program correctness — Hoare logic for sequential reasoning and separation logic for heap and pointer reasoning — taught with live F*/Pulse proofs as the working environment, bridging formal theory and mechanised practice.

Foundations of Formal Verification and Mathematical Logic
Undergraduate / Postgraduate

Teaches formal verification the way it is actually practised — starting from "what could go wrong in this program?" and building toward the mathematics that answers it precisely, rather than starting from symbol systems and hoping intuition follows.

Data Structures and Algorithm Design
Undergraduate / Postgraduate

Teaches students to see through the structure of a problem before touching an implementation — recognising that the choice of data structure is fundamentally an efficiency decision, and that algorithm design is fundamentally a correctness decision. Students learn to make both choices deliberately and justify them, not by pattern-matching to familiar solutions but by reasoning from the shape of the problem itself.

Cryptography — Algorithms, Mathematical Principles and Protocol Correctness
Postgraduate / Professional

Teaches cryptographic algorithms through dual lenses of efficiency and correctness — not just how protocols work, but how to specify precisely what "secure" and "correct" mean, and what it takes to close every gap in that specification. Directly grounded in SPDM protocol verification work at Microsoft Research Bangalore.

How to Build a Verified System — From Research Idea to Published Proof
Postgraduate / Research

A research methodology course built around the complete intellectual journey of verifying a garbage collector for OCaml — how to see through a complex systems problem to its formal structure, where specifications have gaps, where proofs break, and what it takes to close every gap until the machine accepts the proof.

Teaching Philosophy

Formal verification has a reputation for being impenetrable. My teaching starts from the other direction: concrete programs, vivid analogies, and the question "what could go wrong?" — and only then introduces the mathematics that answers it. Every abstract concept earns its place by solving a problem the student already cares about.

The worked examples I use in teaching are drawn from real, published proofs, not textbook toy problems. Students see the gap between textbook and practice from day one.
